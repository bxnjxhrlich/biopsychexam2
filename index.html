Exam 2 — Electrical / Chemical / Methods

Action Potential

Step 1 — threshold → voltage-gated Na⁺ open → Na⁺ rushes IN (depolarize)
Step 2 — Na⁺ channels INACTIVATE at peak
Step 3 — voltage-gated K⁺ open → K⁺ rushes OUT (repolarize)
Step 4 — brief hyperpolarization
Step 5 — Na/K pump returns to rest
Absolute refractory — Na⁺ inactivated; NO AP possible
Relative refractory — hyperpolarized; AP possible but harder
Na/K pump — 3 Na⁺ OUT, 2 K⁺ IN (uses ATP)
Why no backwards prop — behind is in absolute refractory
Speed — bigger axon = faster · myelination = faster (saltatory)
Fastest — large myelinated · Slowest — small unmyelinated
EPSP — depolarizing (excitatory)
IPSP — hyperpolarizing (inhibitory)
Summation — at axon hillock; spatial (different sites) + temporal (close in time)

Synapse

Step 1 Exocytosis — AP → voltage-gated Ca²⁺ open → Ca²⁺ enters → vesicle fusion (REQUIRES Ca²⁺)
Step 2 Release — NT into cleft
Step 3 Binding — NT to postsyn receptors → EPSP/IPSP
Step 4 Deactivation — reuptake, enzymatic degradation, diffusion
Otto Loewi (1921) — frog heart → proved chemical neurotransmission; "vagusstoff" = ACh
Electrical synapse (gap junction) — FASTER, no delay
Chemical synapse — slower but flexible/modulatory
Ionotropic — FASTER; channel = part of receptor (e.g., ACh nicotinic)
Metabotropic — slower; G-protein → 2nd messenger; long-term effects (e.g., NE)

Exam 3 — Tolerance / Stress / Emotion

Tolerance & Withdrawal

Tolerance — ↓ response over time; curve shifts RIGHT
Metabolic tolerance — body better at metabolizing drug
Functional tolerance — cellular (receptor downreg) OR conditioned (Pavlovian)
Cross-tolerance — tolerance to one drug → tolerance to similar drug
Sensitization — opposite of tolerance; curve shifts LEFT (cocaine, amphetamine)
Withdrawal — opposite of drug effects
Opiate effect — drowsy, constipation, ↓respiration, ↓HR
Opiate withdrawal — insomnia, diarrhea, ↑respiration, ↑HR/BP
Alcohol withdrawal — more severe than opiates; can be DEADLY; lasts weeks
Alcohol w/d over-excitation — chronic alcohol = GABA agonist + glutamate antagonist → brain compensates (↓ GABA receptors, ↑ glutamate receptors) → withdrawal = too little inhibition + too much excitation → seizures
Conditioned tolerance evidence — Siegel: rats overdose on usual morphine dose in unfamiliar environments; max tolerance in drug-paired env
Pavlovian model — env (CS) + drug (US) → conditioned compensatory response opposite to drug effect

Drug Classes (BLUE drugs starred)

Caffeine — adenosine antagonist
★ Nicotine — ACh agonist
★ Cocaine — DA agonist (blocks DA reuptake)
★ Amphetamine — DA agonist (↑ release + blocks reuptake)
★ Alcohol — GABA agonist + glutamate antagonist
★ Valium (benzo) — GABA agonist (positive allosteric mod of GABA-A)
★ Buspar — 5-HT agonist
★ Morphine (heroin, methadone, fentanyl, codeine) — opiate agonist
★ LSD — 5-HT agonist
Marijuana — cannabinoid agonist · Mescaline / MDMA — 5-HT · Ketamine — NMDA antagonist

Cocaine

Short-term — ↑ energy, ↓ appetite, mental alertness, ↑ HR/BP, constricted vessels, ↑ temp, dilated pupils
Long-term — addiction, irritability, restlessness, paranoia, auditory hallucinations
Medical complications — arrhythmias, heart attacks, chest pain, respiratory failure, strokes, seizures, headaches, abdominal pain, nausea
Cellular effect — blocks DA reuptake → DA stays in synapse longer
Pathway — mesocorticolimbic (VTA → nucleus accumbens)
Chronic abusers — ↓ DA + ↓ DA receptors

Reward / ICSS / Microdialysis

Olds & Milner (1954) — rats lever-press 1000s/hr for self-stimulation; cross electrified floor; ignore food/water/sleep
ICSS sites — hypothalamus, septum, nucleus accumbens, VTA, medial forebrain bundle (most effective)
DA-ICSS-addiction — sites overlap mesolimbic DA pathway; ICSS → ↑ DA in nucleus accumbens; DA agonists ↑ ICSS; DA antagonists block; lesions of mesolimbocortical pathway block
"Wanting vs liking" — addiction = ↑ wanting (not necessarily liking)
Microdialysis — probe in brain → samples → HPLC measures NT concentration
Conditioned place preference — drug-paired chamber preferred → drug was rewarding; DA agonists produce CPP

Stress & HPA Axis

Stress definition — any deviation of the body from homeostasis
Hans Selye — first to systematically study stress; suggested stress → illness
3 stress systems — endocrine (HPA), autonomic (sympathetic), behavioral (brain)
HPA axis — hypothalamus → CRF → pituitary → ACTH → adrenal cortex → cortisol
Cortisol feedback — negatively feeds back, inhibits HPA
Cortisol effects — ↑ glucose synthesis, ↓ glucose uptake into fat/muscle, breaks down fat, suppresses immune system, affects fear behavior
Cushing's Syndrome — hypercortisolism (any cause incl. steroid meds)
Cushing's Disease — pituitary tumor → ↑ ACTH → ↑ cortisol
Sympathetic effects — ↑ blood to muscles, ↑ HR, ↑ blood sugar, piloerection; releases adrenaline (epinephrine) from adrenal glands
Both endocrine + autonomic — CATABOLIC — mobilize energy (glucose, fat)

Emotion Theories

Darwin observed — expressions same in animals, young children, born blind → INNATE
Darwin concluded — expressions evolved from behaviors signaling next action; opposite messages = opposite behaviors; retained if helpful for survival
James-Lange — physiology FIRST → interpretation = emotion ("I run → I'm afraid")
Cannon-Bard — physiology + emotion SIMULTANEOUSLY
Data vs James-Lange — epinephrine injection ≠ emotion; ppl w/o peripheral sensation still feel emotion

Fear & Amygdala

Fear in rodents (signs) — freezing, ↓ HR, ↓ salivation, ↑ startle, defecation, hypoalgesia, ↓ activity
Structure for conditioned fear — amygdala
Amygdala damage evidence — every fear measure impaired (rabbit, rat, mouse, humans w/ Urbach-Wiethe)
Basolateral complex (BLA + LA) — INPUT; where CS-US LEARNING happens
Central nucleus (CE) — OUTPUT; coordinates fear response (HR via dMNV, freezing via PAG, startle via PnC)
Kluver-Bucy (medial temporal lobe damage) — emotional blunting, hyperphagia, inappropriate sexual behavior, visual agnosia
Extinction — present CS w/o US → fear ↓; NOT forgetting, IS new learning (CS now safe)
Extinction requires — amygdala (turning off blocks extinction)
D-cycloserine — facilitates extinction (NMDA partial agonist) → adjunct to therapy (elevator phobia, acrophobia)

Learned Helplessness

Paradigm — 2 groups, SAME amount shock; only diff = controllability (turn wheel to stop)
What's manipulated — controllability
Effects (BLUE) — less aggressive, ↓ social dominance, ↓ eating/drinking, don't interact, ↑ fear learning, ↑ ulcers
Other effects — less responsive to shock, less struggling, analgesia, learning impairments, anxiety, drugs more rewarding
As depression model — for — shares features (anhedonia, withdrawal, ulcers, anxiety); antidepressants reverse it
As depression model — flaws — misses negative thinking, suicidality
Maier's interpretation — models LACK OF CONTROL over stressors (one component of depression/anxiety/PTSD)
Uncontrollability → fight/flight — ↓ escape
Uncontrollability → fear/anxiety — ↑
Autoreceptor — inhibitory presynaptic receptor; regulates own NT release; negative feedback
DRN role — activation = NECESSARY AND SUFFICIENT for LH
DRN evidence — uncontrollable shock activates DRN 5-HT > controllable
DRN sensitization — ↓ 5-HT1A autoreceptor function → less neg feedback → more 5-HT release
mPFC role — inhibits DRN; controllable stress activates mPFC → no LH
mPFC critical fact — shutting OFF mPFC = LH even with control
mPFC functions — executive control, inhibits lower regions, emotion regulation

Anxiety & BNST

Anxiety brain hypothesis — amygdala overactive (PTSD fMRI confirms)
BNST = bed nucleus of stria terminalis
Amygdala vs BNST (Walker & Davis) — amygdala = phasic, predictable, short-duration, cued FEAR; BNST = sustained, unpredictable, long-duration, diffuse ANXIETY
BNST critical for — light-enhanced startle, CRF-enhanced startle, learned helplessness
Goode & Maren (2017) — BNST organizes responses to stimuli that poorly predict WHEN danger occurs
BNST in humans — anticipatory anxiety, sustained unpredictable threat, hypervigilant monitoring
Allosteric modulator — binds DIFFERENT site from NT; changes how NT activates receptor
Valium mechanism — positive allosteric mod of GABA-A → makes GABA more effective → less anxiety
5-HT in anxiety — CONFUSING; depends on receptor subtype, chronic vs acute, brain region; 5-HT1A KO mice → ↑ anxiety

Antidepressants & Depression

MAOIs (iproniazid) — block MAO → ↑ all monoamines (DA, NE, 5-HT); MOST SEVERE side effects (tyramine → hypertensive crisis)
Tricyclics (TCAs) (imipramine) — block reuptake of NE + 5-HT; anticholinergic, cardiac, sedation
SSRIs (Prozac, Zoloft, Luvox) — block 5-HT reuptake only; mildest side effects
Monoamine theory — tryptophan depletion → depressive sx; PET shows ↓ 5-HT receptor binding
Genetic — ~40% twin concordance
Stress-diathesis hypothesis — HPA hyperactivity, ↑ CRF in depressed; SSRIs may normalize
Neurogenic theory — hippocampus impaired in depression; chronic antidepressants ↑ hippocampal neurogenesis

Color & Lateral Inhibition

Trichromatic (Young-Helmholtz) — 3 cone types tuned to different wavelengths
Trichromatic incomplete — doesn't explain afterimages or "no reddish-green"
Opponent-process — paired opposites: red-green, blue-yellow, black-white; retina has red-green + blue-yellow color ganglion cells
Opponent-process explains — afterimages
Opponent-process incomplete — doesn't fully explain color constancy
Negative afterimage — stare at color → see opposite (selective adaptation)
Color constancy — recognize color despite lighting changes
Retinex theory — cortex compares retinal areas to determine color/brightness; explains constancy
Color blindness most common — red & green cones share same photopigment
Color blindness X-linked — 8% men, 1% women → MUCH more common in men
Lateral inhibition — active neuron inhibits neighbors
Lateral inhibition function — sharpens contrasts, emphasizes borders → perceive shapes
Receptive field — part of visual field that excites/inhibits a neuron

Visual Cortex

Path — retina → optic nerve → optic chiasm (cross midline) → optic tract → LGN (thalamus) → V1
Right visual field — → LEFT V1
LGN magnocellular — large neurons, mostly rods, movement
LGN parvocellular — small neurons, mostly cones, color, details, slow/non-moving
V1 (primary visual cortex) — first cortical visual processing; conscious vision
Blindsight — V1 damage → can react to visual info without consciously seeing it (subcortical pathways)
Visual association cortex — further processes shape, movement, brightness, color
Feature detectors — neurons respond to specific features (shape, depth, movement); more complex shapes as info processed
Selective adaptation — prolonged exposure → tuned neurons ↓ firing; supports feature-detector idea
Simple cells — in V1; FIXED excitatory + inhibitory zones in RF; specific orientation, specific location
Complex cells — respond to particular orientation ANYWHERE in large RF (no fixed zones)
Complex shapes — inferior temporal cortex (receives from fovea)
Faces — fusiform gyrus (in IT cortex); also greebles, dog faces, car brands w/ expertise

Circadian / SCN

Circadian rhythm — daily rhythmic changes in behavior/physiology
Entrainment by light — natural rhythm synced to day/night
Without day/night — PERSISTS, free-runs near 24 hrs (internal clock)
SCN = suprachiasmatic nucleus
SCN location — hypothalamus, above optic chiasm
SCN evidence (1) — oscillates without input (single-cell oscillations)
SCN evidence (2) — receives DIRECT input from retina
SCN evidence (3) — lesions destroy rhythms (no recovery)
SCN evidence (4) — transplants restore rhythms → chemical signal exists
SCN active when — DAY (regardless of nocturnal/diurnal)

Sleep — 2 Drives, Stages, REM

2 processes drive sleep — sleep debt (homeostatic) + circadian rhythms in arousal
Sleep debt — diff btwn sleep needed and got; builds while awake
Awake alert — Beta (desynchronized)
Awake quiet (eyes closed) — Alpha (8–12 Hz), regular
Stage 1 — Theta (3.5–7.5 Hz); transition
Stage 2 — Theta + sleep spindles (12–14 Hz, 2–5/min) + K-complexes (only here, ~1/min, can be triggered by noise)
Stages 3–4 — Delta < 3.5 Hz; slow-wave; deepest, hardest to wake
REM — looks like Stage 1 (desynchronized)
REM features — eyes dart back & forth, muscle atonia (motor neuron inhibition in spinal cord), more easily awakened, alert when up, story-like dreams
REM & sleep paralysis — atonia can persist if you wake during REM
Progression — 90-min cycles; SWS dominates EARLY; REM gets LONGER through night

Sleep Brain Regions

Brainstem RAS — arousal control; many regions critical for REM (EEG activation + atonia)
Cholinergic neurons — fire in anticipation of waking + REM
VLPO (ventrolateral preoptic) — "sleep neurons" — GABA inhibits arousal neurons elsewhere
Preoptic lesions — complete insomnia → lethal exhaustion in days

Sleep Neurotransmitters

Adenosine — builds during day, INHIBITS arousal → sleep-promoting; caffeine blocks adenosine receptors
Histamine (post hypothal) — arousal; antihistamines → drowsy; REM OFF
Orexin (lat hypothal) — arousal, broad excitation; degeneration → narcolepsy
ACh — arousal + REM; hippocampal memory; REM ON (high in waking AND REM)
NE (locus coeruleus) — arousal, vigilance; high awake, low SWS, ABSENT REM; REM OFF
5-HT (raphe) — cortical arousal; automatic movements; REM OFF

Why Sleep?

Universal in — vertebrates
Sleep deprivation studies — equivocal
Body rest — NOT necessary (body OK without sleep, can do physical tasks)
Brain rest — SWS = low metabolism; clears free radicals
Brain development — REM peaks during dev (70% infant sleep is REM)
Memory — REM = procedural learning; SWS = declarative learning
Sleep-deprived rats — weak, uncoordinated, can't regulate temp, hyperphagic but lose weight, eventually die

Learning & Memory

Learning — long-lasting behavior change from experience
Memory — record of experience underlying learning
Observe learning? — NOT directly; only see performance
Performance change ≠ learning — fatigue, motivation, growth, drugs can change perf without learning
HM surgery — bilateral medial temporal lobe (Scoville 1953) for epilepsy
HM removed — ~⅔ of hippocampal formation, parts of temporal cortex, amygdala
HM effect — severe ANTEROGRADE amnesia; couldn't form new long-term memories
HM intact — pre-surgery memory + short-term memory
HM PRESERVED memory — PROCEDURAL
HM tasks OK on — mirror drawing, rotary pursuit, incomplete pictures, Pavlovian conditioning
HM tasks FAIL — word lists, recognizing experimenter, recent news (any conscious recall)
Retrograde amnesia — loss of events BEFORE injury
Anterograde amnesia — loss of events AFTER injury
Declarative (explicit) — conscious; people, places, events
Procedural (implicit) — unconscious; tying shoes, hitting ball, speaking
Memory consolidation — weak new memory → stronger, less disruptable over time
LTP — long-term potentiation
LTP discovered by — Bliss & Lomo
LTP why interesting — lasting ↑ in synaptic strength after activity ("neurons that fire together, wire together") → cellular model of memory
LTP requires — NMDA receptors (in hippocampus)
NMDA blockade — impairs water-maze learning (Morris)
NMDA receptor — glutamate receptor
NMDA requires 2 events — glutamate binding + depolarization → coincidence detector
NMDA permeable to — Ca²⁺, Na⁺, K⁺
Possible LTP changes — more receptors (AMPA), more NT release, new spines, larger spines

Stroke

Cerebral hemorrhage — bleeding (hypertension, aneurysm rupture, blood disease)
Obstructive (ischemic) — blocked flow
Ischemia — loss of blood flow
Thrombosis — clot/fat blockage AT site
Embolism — blockage MOVES from larger to smaller vessel
Arteriosclerosis — vessel narrowing
Most damage = EXCITOTOXIC (not direct ischemia)
Excitotoxic cascade — blood-deprived neurons overactive → excessive glutamate → NMDA receptors → Ca²⁺ flood → poisons neuron
Symptoms — sudden numbness/weakness one side, sudden confusion / trouble speaking, sudden vision trouble, sudden walking trouble, sudden severe headache
F.A.S.T. — Face droop, Arm drift, Speech slurred, Time (911)
Consequences — cognitive issues, hemiplegia (one-side paralysis), hemiparesis (one-side weakness), speech, emotion, depression, numbness, pain
Recurrence — 25% have another within 5 years
tPA — clot-dissolver; within 3 hrs; can be neurotoxic
DISPA — anticoagulant from vampire bats
Neuroprotectants — glutamate antagonists, antioxidants, apoptosis inhibitors (none FDA-approved)
Also — physical therapy

Seizures & Epilepsy

Seizure — sudden surge of abnormal, excessive, synchronized electrical activity
Aura — peculiar pre-convulsion changes (smells, thoughts, feelings, hallucinations, chest tightness)
Focal/partial — restricted to focus
Simple partial — sensory/motor only (no LOC)
Complex partial — usu. temporal lobes; automatisms + altered consciousness
Generalized — brain-wide, no focal point
Grand mal — tonic phase (no breathing, contraction) → clonic phase (rhythmic contraction) → coma (~5 min)
Petit mal — brief unawareness (absence)
Automatism — involuntary, repetitive motor behavior w/o awareness during seizure (lip-smacking, fidgeting, walking aimlessly); common in complex partial

Parkinson's Disease

Symptom 1 — RIGIDITY (cogwheel — short jerky movements)
Symptom 2 — RESTING TREMOR (hands; disappears in sleep)
Symptom 3 — BRADYKINESIA (slow movement, paucity of speech)
Symptom 4 — POSTURAL INSTABILITY (can't catch self when tripping)
Cause — loss of DA neurons in substantia nigra (midbrain tegmentum)
Pathway lost — nigrostriatal DA pathway (SN → striatum)
MPTP story — 1982 backstreet chemist made MPTP → opiate addicts got Parkinson's-like sx → MPTP destroys SN DA → animal model
Disinhibition — "inhibiting an inhibitor" → net excitation
BG circuit (1) — Cortex → striatum (glutamate, excitatory)
BG circuit (2) — Striatum → GPi (GABA, inhibitory)
BG circuit (3) — GPi → thalamus (GABA, inhibitory)
BG circuit (4) — Thalamus → motor cortex (excitation)
BG circuit (5) — SN → striatum (DA, facilitates)
PD breaks circuit — ↓ DA → striatum can't inhibit GPi → GPi over-inhibits thalamus → no movement
L-Dopa (Levodopa) — DA can't cross BBB, but L-Dopa CAN → converted to DA in brain (mainstay)
Deprenyl (selegiline) — MAO-B inhibitor, ↓ DA breakdown
Other drugs — DA agonists, COMT inhibitors
Cryothalamotomy — destroys tremor-producing thalamic area
DBS — deep brain stimulation (subthalamic nucleus or GPi)
Pallidotomy — interrupts globus pallidus pathway
Transplants — fetal/stem-cell DA neurons (experimental)

Degeneration / Regeneration

Anterograde degeneration — distal axon dies after damage (downstream end goes)
Retrograde degeneration — damage near terminal → upstream cell body dies
Transneuronal — damaged neuron's death triggers connected neurons (anterograde or retrograde transneuronal)
Apoptosis — ACTIVE programmed cell death; chromatin condenses, nucleus fragments, cell shrinks; CLEAN, no inflammation
Necrosis — PASSIVE cell death; cell swells & bursts; messy, inflammation
Regeneration in invertebrates — easy
Regeneration in adult humans — largely lost
Regeneration depends on — extent of damage + distance axons must travel
PNS vs CNS regen — PNS > CNS
Regeneration helped by — Schwann cells, growth factors, cell adhesion molecules
