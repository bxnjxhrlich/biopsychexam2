⚡ A — Action Potentials & Neural Conduction

Resting Membrane Potential

At rest the neuron's membrane potential is ~−70 mV. Potassium (K⁺) has the highest permeability at rest and therefore exerts the most influence on resting membrane potential.
Ion distribution: Extracellular (outside) → Na⁺ and Cl⁻. Intracellular (inside) → K⁺ and large protein anions (P⁻).
Two forces act on every ion: Electrostatic pressure (opposite charges attract, like charges repel) and chemical concentration gradients (ions diffuse from high to low concentration). Together these determine each ion's equilibrium potential.
IonChargeMostly FoundEquilibrium Potential
Potassium (K⁺)+Inside cell~ −85 mV
Sodium (Na⁺)+Outside cell~ +55 mV
Chloride (Cl⁻)−Outside cell~ −66 mV
Calcium (Ca²⁺)+Outside cellPositive (similar pressures to Na⁺)

Ion Channels

Ion channels are protein structures that span the membrane and allow specific ions to pass through. The membrane is "selectively permeable" because of these specialized channels.
Types of ion channel gating (what causes them to open):
  • Leak channels — always open
  • Voltage-gated — open in response to changes in membrane voltage (these mediate action potentials)
  • Chemical / ligand-gated — opened by neurotransmitters, peptides, or hormones
  • Mechanical — opened by physical movement (e.g., touch receptors)
  • Temperature-gated — respond to heat or cold
  • Acidity-gated — respond to pH changes

The Action Potential

An action potential (AP) is a brief, rapid reversal of the membrane potential that travels down the axon as the neuron's electrical signal. It is all-or-none — it either fires completely or not at all.
Action potential waveform showing ion flow, threshold, and refractory periods
Step-by-step:
  1. A stimulus depolarizes the membrane to threshold (~−55 mV)
  2. Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open → Na⁺ rushes IN → rapid depolarization to ~+40 mV
  3. Na⁺ channels quickly inactivate (ball-and-chain mechanism — they can't reopen immediately)
  4. After a slight delay, voltage-gated K⁺ channels open → K⁺ rushes OUT → repolarization back toward −70 mV
  5. K⁺ channels stay open a bit too long → membrane overshoots to ~−90 mV (hyperpolarization)
  6. K⁺ channels close, membrane returns to resting −70 mV

Refractory Periods

TypeCan an AP Fire?Cause (Ion)
Absolute refractoryNO — impossible regardless of stimulus strengthNa⁺ channels are inactivated (cannot reopen yet)
Relative refractoryYES — but requires a stronger-than-normal stimulusMembrane is hyperpolarized (extra K⁺ left) — farther from threshold
Absolute = Absolutely impossible (Na⁺ inactivated). Relative = Requires more stimulus (hyperpolarized by K⁺).

The Sodium-Potassium Pump (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase)

Maintains resting ion concentrations. Pumps 3 Na⁺ OUT of the cell and 2 K⁺ INTO the cell, against their concentration gradients, using ATP energy. This restores/maintains the resting potential.
3 Na out, 2 K in — "Three sodium leave, two potassium arrive."

AP Propagation

The AP propagates down the axon in one direction only. It doesn't go backwards because the region just behind the AP is in its refractory period — Na⁺ channels there are inactivated.
FactorFastestSlowest
Axon diameterLarger (bigger) = fasterSmaller = slower
MyelinationMyelinated = fastest (saltatory conduction)Unmyelinated = slowest (passive spread)
Saltatory conduction: In myelinated axons, the AP "jumps" from one Node of Ranvier to the next, vastly increasing conduction speed. The myelin insulates the axon segments between nodes.

EPSPs, IPSPs, and Summation

EPSP (Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential) = a small depolarization — moves the membrane potential closer to threshold, making the neuron more likely to fire.
IPSP (Inhibitory Postsynaptic Potential) = a small hyperpolarization — moves the membrane potential farther from threshold, making the neuron less likely to fire.
Dendrites and the soma show graded responses (bigger input = bigger change, unlike the all-or-none AP). EPSPs and IPSPs summate — the neuron "adds up" all excitatory and inhibitory inputs arriving at the same time or in rapid succession. If the sum crosses threshold → AP fires.

🔗 B — Synaptic Communication

Otto Loewi's Discovery (1921)

Loewi stimulated the vagus nerve of frog heart #1 → it slowed down. Heart #2, sitting in the same bath solution, also slowed — even though its vagus nerve wasn't stimulated. He concluded the vagus nerve released a chemical substance into the solution, which he called "vagusstoff." Vagusstoff turned out to be acetylcholine (ACh). He was stimulating the parasympathetic branch ("rest and digest") of the autonomic nervous system. Won the 1936 Nobel Prize.

The Synapse

A synapse is the gap between one neuron and the next where neurotransmitter is released and binds to receptors. Neurotransmitters are packaged into vesicles inside the presynaptic terminal.

Steps of Synaptic Transmission

2. Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open

3. Ca²⁺ triggers exocytosis

4. Vesicles fuse, NT spills into synapse

5. NT binds postsynaptic receptors

Exocytosis = the process of vesicles fusing with the presynaptic membrane and spilling their neurotransmitter contents into the synapse. Requires calcium (Ca²⁺)!

Neurotransmitter Deactivation

After release, neurotransmitter is removed from the synapse by:
  • Reuptake — NT is pumped back into the presynaptic neuron (most common mechanism)
  • Enzymatic degradation — enzymes in the synapse break down the NT (e.g., acetylcholinesterase breaks down ACh)
  • Diffusion — NT simply drifts away from the synapse

Receptor Types

Ionotropic receptors open ion channels directly (fast). Metabotropic receptors use G-proteins and second messengers (slow but lasting).
Inhibitory receptor example: GABA — the main inhibitory NT; often acts at ionotropic receptors (GABA-A) that open Cl⁻ channels → hyperpolarization → IPSP.

Non-Invasive Brain Monitoring

CT and regular MRI show anatomy only. EEG, PET, and fMRI can measure brain function/activity.
EEG (Electroencephalography): Measures electrical activity of large groups of neurons using electrodes on the scalp. Used diagnostically for epilepsy, sleep disorders, brain death. Yes — an EEG can accidentally pick up muscle electrical activity (EMG artifact) since muscles also produce electrical signals.
Evoked potential = an EEG signal that is time-locked to a specific stimulus. You present a stimulus repeatedly and average the EEG responses — the random noise cancels out, revealing the brain's specific response to that stimulus.
CT (Computed Tomography): Uses X-rays taken from many angles to create cross-sectional brain images. Shows structure only — useful for detecting tumors, strokes, and structural damage.
PET (Positron Emission Tomography): Inject a radioactive substance. You can inject radioactive 2-deoxyglucose (2-DG) — because active neurons consume more glucose, the radioactive glucose accumulates in active brain regions. This lets you measure functional activity.
MRI vs. fMRI: Regular MRI uses a magnetic field and radio waves to produce detailed images of brain structure (anatomy). Functional MRI (fMRI) measures brain activity by detecting the BOLD signal (Blood Oxygen Level-Dependent) — active brain areas consume more oxygen, which changes the magnetic properties of hemoglobin, and fMRI detects this change.

Invasive Research Techniques

Stereotaxic device: A surgical instrument that holds an animal's head in a fixed position and uses 3D coordinates (from a brain atlas) to guide an electrode, cannula, or probe to a precise location in the brain.

Animal Research Ethics

Arguments FOR animal research: Led to life-saving medical treatments (vaccines, surgical techniques, drug development); animals also benefit (veterinary medicine developed from animal research); research is tightly regulated to ensure humane treatment.
Arguments AGAINST animal research: Ethical concerns about causing suffering to sentient beings; species differences may limit how well results translate to humans; alternative methods (computer models, cell cultures, imaging) are continually improving.
IACUC (Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee): Every institution that conducts animal research must have one. Functions: reviews and approves all animal research protocols before research can begin; conducts inspections of animal facilities; ensures compliance with federal regulations. Must include at least one veterinarian and one community member not affiliated with the institution.
Animal Welfare Act provisions (know the "details of the law" in blue on slides):
  • Minimum standards for housing, feeding, handling, sanitation, ventilation, and veterinary care
  • Researchers must demonstrate that the research is necessary and that alternatives (computer models, cell cultures) are not feasible
  • Pain and distress must be minimized — adequate anesthesia, analgesia, and euthanasia procedures required
  • Annual reporting of animal numbers and procedures to the USDA
  • IACUC oversight is mandated by law

💊 D — Psychopharmacology

Basics

Psychoactive drug = any chemical substance that affects the nervous system (specifically the brain) to alter perception, mood, consciousness, or behavior.
Drugs commonly act on the nervous system (especially at synapses) to affect behavior. To reach the brain, drugs must enter the bloodstream and cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB).
Once in the blood, drugs can be distributed throughout the body very quickly (seconds to minutes) because the circulatory system reaches every tissue.

Routes of Drug Administration

RouteSpeedNotes
Intravenous (IV)⚡ FastestInjected directly into the bloodstream
Inhalation⚡ Very fastLungs have huge surface area → rapid absorption
Intramuscular (IM)ModerateInjected into muscle tissue
Subcutaneous (SC)ModerateInjected under the skin
Oral🐢 SlowestMust be absorbed through GI tract; undergoes first-pass metabolism in liver
Key trade-off: Faster routes produce a quicker, more intense effect but do NOT maintain drug blood levels as long as slower routes. Slower routes (like oral) produce a more gradual, sustained effect.

Blood-Brain Barrier & Drug Access

Lipid solubility determines whether a drug can cross the BBB. The BBB is made of tightly packed cells with a lipid (fat) membrane — so fat-soluble drugs cross easily while water-soluble ones cannot.
Why heroin is more potent than morphine: Heroin is more lipid-soluble than morphine, so it crosses the BBB faster and in greater quantities → more intense effect.
Why some antihistamines don't cause drowsiness: Newer ("non-drowsy") antihistamines are designed to be less lipid-soluble, so they cannot cross the BBB effectively. They work on histamine receptors in the body (reducing allergy symptoms) but don't reach the brain (so no sedation).
Yes, there are psychoactive drugs that do NOT cross the BBB — these only affect the peripheral nervous system.

Drug Metabolism

Drugs are commonly broken down (metabolized) by enzymes in the liver.
Acetaldehyde = a toxic intermediate metabolite produced when the body breaks down alcohol (ethanol). Normally, a second enzyme quickly converts acetaldehyde into harmless acetic acid.
Antabuse (disulfiram) treats alcoholism by blocking the enzyme that breaks down acetaldehyde. Result: when the person drinks, acetaldehyde accumulates → causes severe nausea, vomiting, headaches → aversive experience deters drinking.

4 Rules That Define a Neurotransmitter

A substance is considered a neurotransmitter if it is:
  1. Synthesized in the neuron
  2. Released from the neuron when the neuron is stimulated
  3. Binds to receptors on the postsynaptic cell and produces a biological effect
  4. Deactivated — removed or broken down after release

Agonists & Antagonists

Agonist = a drug that mimics or enhances a neurotransmitter's effect (activates the receptor or increases NT activity). Antagonist = a drug that blocks or reduces a neurotransmitter's effect (binds to receptor without activating it, preventing the real NT from binding).

Mechanisms of Drug Action (Starred on Slides)

Drugs can alter synaptic function by:
  • Increasing or decreasing NT synthesis (precursor supply)
  • Blocking storage/transport of NT into vesicles
  • Stimulating or blocking NT release
  • Directly activating postsynaptic receptors (agonist)
  • Directly blocking postsynaptic receptors (antagonist)
  • Blocking reuptake — NT stays in synapse longer (e.g., SSRIs block serotonin reuptake; cocaine blocks DA reuptake)
  • Blocking enzymatic degradation — NT isn't broken down (e.g., MAO inhibitors prevent monoamine breakdown)

Dose-Response Curves

Blue = normal dose-response. Red dashed = with tolerance (shifted right — need more drug for same effect).
Dose-response curve shows the relationship between drug dose and the magnitude of its effect. Different effects of the same drug can have different dose-response curves (e.g., therapeutic effect vs. side effect may kick in at different doses).
In the presence of a full agonist, the asymptote (plateau) of the curve often represents maximum receptor activation — adding more drug beyond this point produces no additional effect because all receptors are occupied.
Drug tolerance = diminished response to a drug after repeated use. It shifts the dose-response curve to the right — you need a higher dose to achieve the same effect.
